Methods

We quantify climate-driven changes in agricultural production potential on a global grid at 5 arc-minute resolution (approximately 10×10 km at the equator). For each grid cell, we take the cropping pattern observed at approximately 2020—which crops are grown and how much area each occupies—and hold it fixed across all time periods and scenarios. We then calculate how the attainable yield of these crops changes under different climate conditions. By keeping the crop mix constant while only climate varies, we isolate the direct impact of climate change on agricultural productivity.

Conceptual Framework

Agricultural output in any location depends on three factors: (1) climate conditions (temperature, precipitation, growing season characteristics), (2) cropping patterns (which crops are grown and how much area each occupies), and (3) agronomic management (input intensity, irrigation, technology).

Our approach isolates the climate component by holding the other two factors constant:

  • Fixed cropping pattern: The distribution of crops and harvested areas is held constant across all time periods and scenarios—both historical baselines and future projections. This is the "no-adaptation" assumption: farmers are assumed not to switch crops, expand into new areas, or adopt new technologies in response to changing climate.
  • Fixed management assumption: We use GAEZ attainable yield estimates at a consistent management level (high-input, with limited mechanisation) across all scenarios. For each grid cell, irrigated and rainfed production are combined using the observed harvested area shares.

With this design, any differences in production across time periods or scenarios reflect pure climate-driven changes in yield potential—not adaptation through crop switching, irrigation expansion, or technological change. This establishes a baseline against which the scale of the adaptation challenge can be measured.

Data Sources

Attainable yields: We use GAEZ v5 gridded attainable yield estimates (Theme 5), produced jointly by FAO and IIASA. Attainable yield represents the maximum productivity achievable given the climate and soil conditions of each grid cell, assuming a specified management level. It accounts for climate factors (temperature, precipitation, solar radiation, growing season), soil characteristics (nutrients, texture, depth, drainage), terrain constraints (slope, elevation), and crop physiology and growth requirements. Attainable yield is adjusted downward from theoretical "potential yield" (Theme 2) to reflect realistic site-specific soil and terrain limitations.

Cropping patterns: Harvested area data comes from GAEZ v5 Theme 6, which provides the spatial distribution of crops and harvested areas based on downscaled agricultural statistics reflecting observed cropping patterns at approximately year 2020. These cropping patterns are held constant across all time periods and climate scenarios, so that any differences in production potential reflect only the effect of changing climate.

Climate data: For historical periods, the climate forcing comes from the AgERA5 reanalysis dataset, which provides observed (reconstructed) climate. For future periods, we use CMIP6 climate model ensemble projections (multi-model mean) under the SSP5-8.5 scenario. GAEZ v5 provides yield estimates for the following time periods:

  • Historical: 1981–2000 and 2001–2020 (driven by AgERA5)
  • Projected: 2021–2040, 2041–2060, 2061–2080, and 2081–2100 (driven by CMIP6 ensembles)

All spatial layers are harmonised to a common 5 arc-minute grid (4320 × 2160 cells globally) with standardised crop definitions before calculation.

Cell-Level Caloric Production

Let \(A_{i,c}\) denote the harvested area (ha) of crop \(c\) in cell \(i\), held fixed across all periods. Let \(y_{i,c}(s,t)\) denote the GAEZ attainable yield (tonnes/ha) for crop \(c\) in cell \(i\) under climate scenario \(s\) and time period \(t\). Let \(\kappa_c\) denote the caloric content of crop \(c\) (kcal per tonne).

Total caloric production in cell \(i\) is:

$$C_i(s,t) = \sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}} A_{i,c} \cdot y_{i,c}(s,t) \cdot \kappa_c$$

where \(\mathcal{C}\) is the set of food crops. Cash crops (cocoa, coffee, tea, cotton, tobacco, rubber) and residual categories (fodder, crops n.e.s.) are assigned zero caloric value and do not contribute to this measure. Caloric conversion factors are derived from the USDA National Nutrient Database.

People Fed Yearly (PFY)

We convert annual caloric production to People Fed Yearly (PFY) using a reference daily requirement \(c_{\min}\) (kcal/person/day):

$$\text{PFY}_i(s,t) = \frac{C_i(s,t)}{c_{\min} \cdot 365}$$

In practice we use \(c_{\min}=2000\) kcal/person/day as a standard reference value. PFY provides an intuitive measure: it tells us how many people the agricultural output of a cell could feed for a year.

Change Metrics

Let \((s_0,t_0)\) denote the baseline scenario and time period. We report two complementary metrics:

  • Absolute change: \(\Delta C_i(s,t) = C_i(s,t) - C_i(s_0,t_0)\), expressed in People Fed Yearly. This highlights where the largest total losses occur.
  • Percentage change: \(\Delta C_i(s,t) \;/\; C_i(s_0,t_0)\). This highlights where losses are most severe relative to existing production.

On the website, observed changes compare the 2001–2020 period to the 1981–2000 baseline. Projected changes compare future periods (2021–2040 through 2081–2100) to the 2001–2020 baseline. See Why We Use High-Emissions Climate Projections for the rationale behind the choice of SSP5-8.5.
